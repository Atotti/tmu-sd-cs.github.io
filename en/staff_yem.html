Tactile display on fingertips

The aim of this research is to reproduce any touch sensation such as fluffy, gritty, and slick by selectively generating skin receptors. On the other hand, visual displays have been actively developed for showing the recorded pictures or videos with high quality. In contrast, the development of tactile display has only been achieved in a limited range of sensation. It is known that, our tactile sensations are the result of the activation of the skin sensation receptors responding to pressure, low-frequency vibration, high-frequency vibration and shear deformation; and tendon receptors responding to external forces. This study is developing the method that can stimulate these receptors directly by applying vibration to the skin or flowing high voltage current.

If the tactile display is successful as well as the visual display, we can perceive tactile sensation as if we touch the real object even when we are touching the monitor’s screen displaying the images. This technology can be applied in virtual reality (VR) games or online shopping, etc.

Remote collaborative system with vibro-vestibular feedback

Various tasks, such as security patrolling or supervision of construction sites, require the worker to drive a vehicle to reduce their workload. To increase the accuracy of investigation of a scene, remote collaboration with an expert could be useful. In this study, we are developing a collaborative system in which an expert can perceive the motion (i.e., vibration and vestibular sensation) of the vehicle, with realistic sensations.
